            <h1 class="citation__title"><a href="/do/10.1044/2021-0208-snf-recovery-trifecta/full/">After Multiple Blows, ASHA Members in SNFs Face Prolonged Recovery</a></h1>
            <div class="citation__subtitle">
            </div>
        </div>
       <div class="podcast__authors blog-podcast__authors">
        
		<span>By</span><ul aria-label="authors" class="rlist--inline loa comma">
			
			<li>
			    
<span class="hlFld-ContribAuthor">
    <a href="/action/doSearch?relatedAuthorsFacetField=Sarah Warren">
        <span>Sarah Warren</span>

    <sup></sup>

      <div class="podcast_content-wrapper">  
       		<p>COVID-19, PDPM, and Medicare Part B cuts have hit ASHA members working in the skilled nursing facility (SNF) and contract rehabilitation industries hard over the past year and a half. Although the growing distribution of FDA-approved vaccines has given promise to an end to the pandemic, ASHA members working in SNFs face a more prolonged recovery.</p>
<p>Representatives of the National Association of Support in Long-Term Care (NASL) recently informed ASHA that a return to &ldquo;normal&rdquo; for SNF employees looks to be about three to five years away. NASL further identified three factors threatening these industries&rsquo; financial viability:</p>
<ul>
<li>Medicare&rsquo;s <a href="https://www.asha.org/practice/reimbursement/medicare/medicare-patient-driven-payment-model">Patient-Driven Payment Model (PDPM)</a>, a new SNF payment system that took effect Oct. 1, 2019.</li>
<li>The COVID-19 pandemic, which has reduced the patient census in many facilities and impeded service provision because of viral transmission risk.</li>
<li><a href="/do/10.1044/2020-1228-med-cut-reduced/full/">Medicare cut Part B payments for therapy services</a>, effective Jan. 1, 2021. Although those cuts were not as drastic as anticipated, <a href="https://www.asha.org/siteassets/uploadedfiles/practice/reimbursement/medicare/2021-medicare-fee-schedule-for-speech-language-pathologists.pdf">reimbursement has fallen by an average of 3&ndash;4%.</a></li>
</ul>
<p>SNF and rehab companies responded with cost-cutting strategies that affect all rehabilitation disciplines (speech-language pathology and occupational and physical therapy). They eliminated positions, reduced hours, and reduced salaries. Industry representatives indicate that more SNF and rehab company layoffs and salary reductions are likely.</p>
<p>However, ASHA is attempting to understand the projected downturn in the industry, especially in the context of recent media reports that the <a href="https://www.cbsnews.com/news/genesis-healthcare-ceo-george-hager-bonus-exit/">CEO of a SNF chain received a $5.2 million bonus</a> in late 2020. ASHA continues to fight for the preservation of SLPs&rsquo; jobs and salaries during the pandemic.</p>
<h5><strong>PDPM</strong></h5>
<p>PDPM bases payment on the patient&rsquo;s clinical characteristics rather than on therapy minutes&mdash;as the previous system did. It is designed to address concerns that, under the former system, facilities and rehab companies inappropriately provided unnecessary therapy services to increase reimbursement.</p>
<p>Analysis of the first and second quarters of the PDPM transition indicate that SNFs reduced therapy services. According to long-term care industry representatives, average therapy minutes dropped from about 720 to about 600 per patient per week after the transition to PDPM. However, SNFs that effectively incorporated SLPs into the care planning process for patients, saw <a href="/do/10.1044/leader.FTR1.25112020.48/full/">15% higher reimbursement</a> than SNFs that failed to include SLPs.</p>
<p>With reduced therapy minutes, some facilities and rehab companies responded by <a href="/do/10.1044/slps-in-snfs-share-stories-of-pdpm-related-layoffs-treatment-and-pay-cuts/full/#:~:text=In%20the%20past%20week%2C%20SLPs,1.">laying off SLPs or reducing their salaries and hours</a> before and during the early implementation of PDPM. ASHA and the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) maintain that SNFs have an obligation to provide all clinically appropriate services, regardless of reimbursement method, and that a failure to do so puts patients in jeopardy. Failing to provide these services also ignores the positive financial impact SLPs can have within PDPM: Their services can prevent costly hospital readmissions and facility-acquired conditions such as aspiration pneumonia that affect a facility&rsquo;s quality ratings.</p>
<p>However, analysis of PDPM&rsquo;s impact was halted by COVID-19, because the pandemic changed the types and mechanisms of service provision. ASHA remains committed to examining the impact of PDPM after the pandemic subsides. ASHA will identify any problematic trends and bring them to CMS&rsquo; attention to improve the payment system and ensure that SLPs&rsquo; value in SNFs is appropriately recognized.</p>
<h5><strong>COVID-19</strong></h5>
<p>The therapy needs of SNF residents didn&rsquo;t change with the pandemic, but service provision changed because of safety precautions. Some facilities restricted the number and types of clinicians that could enter a facility and stopped providing certain services to minimize the risk of transmission. At the same time, the patient population dropped in many facilities, as people were hesitant to enter a SNF for fear of contracting COVID-19. Also, with elective surgeries on hold, the number of people needing rehab services dropped.</p>
<p>SNF industry representatives indicate a drop in patient census as high as 30% in 2020, leading to significant reduction in revenue and staffing needs. SLPs have felt a corresponding impact on their employment.</p>
<p>ASHA continues to <a href="https://www.asha.org/practice/reimbursement/medicare/providing-telehealth-services-under-medicare-during-the-covid-19-pandemic/">advocate for expansion of Medicare telehealth reimbursement</a> in SNFs, in part to maintain SNF residents&rsquo; access to therapy services. Although a Medicare rule ultimately allowed (limited) teletherapy, SNFs are not using it widely, due to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Lack of coverage of services for swallowing, cognition, and speech-generating devices.</li>
<li>Challenges outside SNF control&mdash;such as limited broadband access in rural areas.</li>
<li>The need for facilitators to help with telehealth sessions, prohibitive due to costs and lack of staff.</li>
</ul>
<p>Responding to these challenges, ASHA&rsquo;s advocacy has prioritized expanding telehealth services, working with Congress on a permanent telehealth solution, and advising rehab providers on ways to provide telepractice services without facilitators.</p>
<h5><strong>Medicare payment reductions in 2021</strong></h5>
<p><a href="/do/10.1044/2020-1228-med-cut-reduced/full/">A law finalized in late December 2020</a> mitigated reductions to outpatient Medicare Part B payments, at least through 2021 (ASHA and other stakeholders advocated vigorously for the reductions in cuts). This year, ASHA and other stakeholders will redouble their fight against further cuts in 2022.</p>
<h5><strong>Looking ahead</strong></h5>
<p>ASHA will take the following steps in 2021 to mitigate&mdash;as much as it can&mdash;the negative impact of the squeeze on SNFs:</p>
<ul>
<li>Maintain pressure on CMS and Congress to stop implementation of additional payment reductions.</li>
<li>Advocate for Medicare policies that reimburse PPE and telehealth services appropriately.</li>
<li>Advocate against salary reductions and layoffs of SLPs in SNFs and engage the SNF and rehabilitation industry to demonstrate that fully integrating speech-language services improves their bottom line and improves quality of care.</li>
<li>Monitor the second wave of PDPM implementation post-pandemic to flag and tackle misuse and to show decision-makers the value of speech-language pathology services.</li>
</ul>
